What is Calculus?

A Brief History

Calculus was independently developed in the late 17th century by Sir Isaac Newton and Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz. Newton needed it to describe planetary motion and physics, while Leibniz developed it to solve optimization problems. Their work built upon earlier contributions from mathematicians like Fermat, Descartes, and others who worked on tangent lines and areas under curves.

The Two Main Branches

Derivatives (Differential Calculus)

Derivatives measure instantaneous rates of change. Think of it as finding the exact speed of a car at a specific moment, or the steepness of a curve at a particular point.

The derivative of $f(x) = x^2$ is $f'(x) = 2x$
This tells us the slope of the parabola at any point $x$.

Integrals (Integral Calculus)

Integrals measure accumulation. They help us find areas under curves, total distances traveled, or the total amount of something that's changing over time.

$\int_0^2 x^2 \, dx = \frac{8}{3}$
This gives us the area under the curve $y = x^2$ from $x = 0$ to $x = 2$.

Real-World Applications

  • Physics: Describing motion, forces, and energy
  • Engineering: Optimizing designs and analyzing systems
  • Economics: Marginal cost, profit maximization
  • Medicine: Drug dosage calculations, population models
  • Technology: Machine learning algorithms, computer graphics

The Purpose of Learning Calculus

Calculus teaches you to think about change and accumulation in precise, mathematical ways. It's the foundation for advanced mathematics, science, and engineering. More importantly, it develops your problem-solving skills and mathematical reasoning.

Partner Investigation: Exploring Derivatives

⏱️ 25 minutes total

Learning Objectives

By the end of this investigation, you will be able to:

  • Describe what a derivative represents in real-world terms
  • Connect derivatives to familiar concepts like speed and slope
  • Identify situations where derivatives are useful

Materials Needed

  • Graphing calculator or access to Desmos (desmos.com/calculator)
  • Investigation handout (provided)
  • Partner to work with

Investigation Overview

You and your partner will explore three different scenarios where the idea of "rate of change" is important. Don't worry about formal definitions or complicated formulas—focus on understanding the concepts and applications.

Scenario 1: The Speeding Car 8 minutes

The Situation: A car's position is given by $s(t) = t^2 + 2t$ meters after $t$ seconds.

Your Task:

  1. Create a table showing the car's position at $t = 0, 1, 2, 3, 4, 5$ seconds
  2. Calculate the average speed between consecutive time intervals
  3. Graph the position function using Desmos or your calculator
  4. Discuss: What do you notice about how the speed is changing?

Think About: If you were in this car, how would you describe the motion to someone? Is the car speeding up or slowing down?

Scenario 2: The Growing Population 8 minutes

The Situation: A bacteria culture grows according to $P(t) = 100e^{0.5t}$ where $P$ is the population after $t$ hours.

Your Task:

  1. Use Desmos to graph this function for $t$ from 0 to 6 hours
  2. Find the population at $t = 0, 1, 2, 3$ hours
  3. Calculate the average rate of population growth between each hour
  4. Discuss: When is the population growing fastest? Slowest?

Think About: If you were a scientist studying this bacteria, what information would be most important to know about its growth rate?

Scenario 3: The Profit Function 8 minutes

The Situation: A company's profit from selling $x$ items is $P(x) = -x^2 + 40x - 300$ dollars.

Your Task:

  1. Graph this function using Desmos (try $x$ from 0 to 50)
  2. Find the profit when selling 10, 20, 30, and 40 items
  3. Identify where profit is increasing vs. decreasing
  4. Estimate where profit appears to be maximized

Think About: If you were the company owner, what would you want to know about how profit changes as you sell more items?

Reflection Questions 1 minute

Jot down quick thoughts on these questions—we'll discuss them as a class:

  1. What do all three scenarios have in common?
  2. Why might it be important to know how quickly something is changing?
  3. Can you think of other real-world situations where rate of change matters?

What You Just Discovered

Congratulations! You've just explored the fundamental concept behind derivatives:

🚗 Speed is the derivative of position

The car's changing speed told you how position was changing at each moment.

🦠 Growth rate is the derivative of population

The bacteria's growth rate told you how quickly the population was changing.

💰 Marginal profit is the derivative of profit

The rate of profit change told you whether selling one more item would increase or decrease profits.

The Big Idea

A derivative measures how quickly one quantity changes with respect to another.

In mathematical terms: If $y = f(x)$, then the derivative $f'(x)$ tells us the instantaneous rate of change of $y$ with respect to $x$.

Why This Matters

Understanding derivatives allows us to:

  • Optimize: Find maximum profits, minimum costs, fastest routes
  • Predict: Determine when populations will grow fastest or when objects will reach maximum height
  • Control: Adjust systems to achieve desired rates of change
  • Understand: Analyze how the world around us changes

Sneak Peek: The Fundamental Theorem of Calculus

Derivatives and integrals are actually inverse operations—they undo each other!

If $F'(x) = f(x)$, then $\int_a^b f(x) \, dx = F(b) - F(a)$

Don't worry about the details yet—we'll build up to this amazing connection!
